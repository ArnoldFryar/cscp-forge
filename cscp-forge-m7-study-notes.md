# Risk, Resilience, and Compliance Study Notes
Generated: 5/9/2026

## Overview
Module 7 asks whether you can recognize supply chain risk exposure, escalation, continuity planning, and governance. Expect scenarios where the right answer is proactive control or resilience design, not late firefighting.

## Key Concepts
- Risk identification and prioritization
- Business continuity and recovery planning
- Control environments and compliance discipline
- Resilience through visibility, redundancy, and response design

## Key Terms
- **Risk register:** A structured record of identified risks, impacts, owners, and mitigation actions.
- **Business continuity plan:** Documented actions and responsibilities for maintaining or restoring operations after disruption.
- **Control:** A preventive, detective, or corrective measure used to reduce risk exposure or consequence.
- **Resilience:** The capacity to anticipate, absorb, and recover from disruption while protecting critical outcomes.

## Frameworks
### Risk prioritization
Rank risks by business consequence and likelihood so mitigation resources go to the real threats.
1. Define the disruption scenario and exposed process or node.
2. Estimate consequence to service, cost, safety, and compliance.
3. Assess likelihood and detectability.
4. Assign mitigation, contingency, and ownership based on priority.

### Continuity response logic
Move from incident detection to service restoration with clear roles and thresholds.
1. Detect the event and classify severity.
2. Escalate through predefined governance.
3. Stabilize critical flow and customer impact first.
4. Recover capability, then capture learning into controls and plans.

## Exam Traps
- Treating risk management as a one-time assessment instead of an ongoing governed process.
- Responding after disruption without building visibility or continuity triggers in advance.
- Selecting extra inventory as the answer when the scenario really points to supplier, process, or governance resilience.

## Manufacturing Example
A sole supplier for a critical component may require dual tooling, inventory buffers, and a tested continuity plan, not only more inspection after a disruption hits.

## Planning Example
If customs compliance failures delay shipments, the right response may be process controls, ownership, and training rather than more emergency freight spend.
